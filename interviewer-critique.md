# Interviewer Critique
## &ranj US Market Entry — Assessment Review

---

### Overall impression

Solid structure. You clearly understand the shape of a paid marketing engagement and the framework you've used (demand creation vs demand capture, working backwards from revenue) shows commercial awareness. That said, there are gaps in the logic that I'd want to push on — particularly around the numbers, channel rationale, and how well the later cases connect back to the strategy you set up in the earlier ones.

Let me go case by case.

---

### Case 1 — The opportunity

**You've listed ABM, demand generation, and demand capture as three separate offerings. How are they related?**

ABM *is* a demand generation strategy. Presenting them as parallel options suggests either you're padding the list or you haven't fully thought through how they connect. I'd want you to explain the relationship — does ABM sit within demand gen, or are you proposing them as alternative approaches depending on budget and ICP clarity?

**Events and conferences are listed as "also recommended" — why are they an afterthought?**

For a B2B company entering a completely new market with zero brand recognition, in-person presence at relevant US industry events (SHRM, ATD, HR Tech) could be the highest-leverage activity in year one. You might be underselling this. What's your thinking on sequencing — do you go paid-first, or do you build brand presence at events before running paid?

**Your "what we need" list is a good start, but it's quite generic.**

Any agency would ask for goals, budget, and ICP. What I'm not seeing:

- What is &ranj's current US brand awareness? Zero recognition in a new market fundamentally changes the strategy.
- What is the average sales cycle length? For enterprise clients, this could be 6-18 months — which has major implications for how you measure success and set expectations.
- What is the current deal size range? You've assumed $500k per client in Case 2, but where does that number come from?
- Has the product been localised for the US market, or is it being sold as-is?

---

### Case 2 — The paid strategy

This is where I have the most questions.

**The budget maths has a problem.**

You've modelled a 1% lead-to-client conversion rate. For a $500k enterprise deal with a cold US audience that has never heard of &ranj, 1% is very optimistic. Enterprise B2B conversion rates from cold lead to closed deal are typically closer to 0.1-0.5%. If the real conversion rate is 0.5%, you need 800 leads, not 400. At 0.1%, you need 4,000 leads and a $4m budget.

Also: $1,000 CPL for enterprise decision-makers in the US on LinkedIn is low. LinkedIn CPC for HR and L&D titles in North America regularly runs $15-30 per click. A qualified lead (someone who fills out a form, not just clicks) could easily cost $2,000-5,000. Have you done any keyword or audience research to validate the $1,000 CPL assumption?

**Why is Meta in the channel mix?**

You've listed Meta alongside Google, LinkedIn, and YouTube. What's the rationale? The ICP for &ranj — HR directors, L&D managers, compliance leads at large enterprises — are not making $500k procurement decisions off the back of Instagram ads. If Meta is in the mix, I want to hear a specific use case for it, not just a channel list.

**The measurement funnel is presented backwards.**

You've written "Sales → Opportunities → MQLs → Leads." That's the goal hierarchy, not the funnel flow. The funnel runs the other way: Leads → MQLs → Opportunities → Sales. Presenting it this way in a client setting would confuse the conversation. Small thing, but it matters.

**"Accept some leakage" — how much is acceptable, and what's the plan to reduce it?**

Attribution in enterprise B2B is genuinely hard, especially across a 12-18 month sales cycle. But "accept leakage" isn't a measurement strategy. What attribution model are you proposing? First touch, last touch, linear? How do you handle offline touchpoints like sales calls and events? What does "capturing as much data at every stage" look like in practice?

**The content idea (Artemis) is creative — but where's the lead capture mechanism?**

Someone plays a moon mission training demo. Then what? How do you convert that engagement into a sales lead? There's no CTA, no lead magnet, no next step proposed. Creative virality without a conversion path is brand spend, not demand generation. How does this sit in the funnel?

### Case 3 — The bot traffic

**A $500 test budget is not a validation strategy for a $400k spend.**

At $500 on Google Ads for enterprise B2B in the US, you might generate 20-50 clicks. That is not statistically meaningful. If you're recommending a test before scaling, what does a *meaningful* test actually look like? What's the minimum spend, duration, and success metric that would give you enough signal to proceed with confidence?

**You haven't asked why there are bots in the first place.**

95% bot traffic is not normal. Was this a targeted bot attack? A badly configured tracking setup? A third-party tool scraping the site? The source of the bots matters — if it's a tracking misconfiguration, you may still have real underlying US interest that's just being mis-attributed. If it's an attack, that's a different problem entirely. Did you address this?

**The pivot to "demand creation over demand capture" is logical — but what does the timeline now look like?**

You've correctly identified that if there's less existing demand than assumed, you need to build it. But demand creation takes 12-24 months to generate pipeline. How does this change the client's expectations and the contract scope? Did you revisit the year 2 revenue target of $2m in light of this? Because if the original target was built on flawed data, the whole business case for the US market entry may need to be reconsidered.

---

### Case 4 — Client management

**"Multiple failures on both sides" — be careful with that framing.**

You've correctly identified that expectation-setting should have happened earlier. But in a call where the client is already frustrated, opening with "both sides made mistakes" can feel defensive and put them on the back foot. The client doesn't want to hear that they failed. How would you frame this in a way that takes ownership without undermining the agency's credibility?

**The first strategy adjustment contradicts Case 3.**

In Case 3, you identified that the bot traffic means real US demand is likely lower than assumed, and that the strategy should shift toward demand creation. In Case 4, your first adjustment is to raise CPA targets on Google Search to generate more leads faster.

If the demand isn't there, increasing Google Search bids won't conjure it. You can't capture demand that doesn't exist. How do you reconcile this? Is the Google Search suggestion a short-term morale move to show the client activity, or do you genuinely believe there's enough search volume to make it viable?

---

### Summary questions I'd want answered

1. What is your actual CPL benchmark for LinkedIn in the US for this ICP? Have you modelled a pessimistic scenario?
2. How long is the expected sales cycle, and how does that affect how you'd report progress to the client in months 1-6?
3. At what point would you recommend &ranj reconsider the US market entry entirely?
4. If you had to cut the channel mix to two channels only in year one, which two and why?
5. What does success look like at 6 months, before any sales have closed?
